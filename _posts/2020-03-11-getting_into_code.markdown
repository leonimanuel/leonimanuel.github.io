I got into coding the day after my 25th birthday. I'd been working on a creative project after graduating college with a business degree and, looking back, part of the motivation for writing that screenplay of mine might well have been to avoid taking the next logical step in my professional career. A while back, I forced myself to hunker down and get back into job pool, it just felt wrong. I wasn't excited about any of the ooportunities that were available, but moreover, even on interviews where things were going well and I would be talking to those whose offices I should be hoping to aspire to down the line, the only attractive thing to me was the sweeping views of those high-rise midtown towers. So, I decided to change things up. I wasn't sure how to change things up, but my siblings were plugged into the coding world and I figured I'd give it a shot. Three days into a Udemy course on JavaScript, I already knew that I was undoubtedly on the right track. 

I started software engineering  because I wanted to try something new, but I'm sticking with it because it gives me access to a part of myself that I had forgotten to attend to in my professional goals. At heart, I'm a builder. That's why I love architecture, and home improvement projects, but most importantly, it's why I love coding. Every moment that I code, I am buiding something, and theres' a surprising amount of creative freedom and ingenuity that the field demands from me on a near-daily basis. It's constantly frustrating, but never a grind. It makes me smarter, more attentive, more patient, and, on Learn's platform, a better communicator. I coud go on, but I have code to write.
